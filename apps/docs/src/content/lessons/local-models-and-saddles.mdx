<Step title="Pick a function shape. Fire a ray. What is this?">
$f(x, y) = a\, x^2 + b\, y^2$. The signs of $a$ and $b$ decide what's happening at the origin. The ray slider sweeps a direction $\theta$; the right panel plots $f$ along that ray.

<SaddleScanner client:visible />

Three shapes worth finding:

1. Both $a$ and $b$ **positive**. Every ray you fire shows the function climbing away from zero. **Local minimum.**
2. Both **negative**. Every ray shows it falling. **Local maximum.**
3. Opposite signs (one positive, one negative). Some rays climb, some fall. There's no consistent "down" or "up." **Saddle.**

The first two cases have analogues you've seen in 1D (a parabola opens up or opens down). The saddle is new. It has no single-variable counterpart, and it's the case that matters most for ML.
</Step>

<Step title="The 1D recall">
Single-variable calculus told you: near a point $a$, a smooth function looks linear. Formally,

$$
f(a + h) \;\approx\; f(a) + f'(a)\, h
$$

when $h$ is small. The derivative $f'(a)$ is the slope of the line that best matches $f$ at $a$. Zoom in far enough and the curve becomes that line.

That's *the* local linear model in 1D. Now we generalize.
</Step>

<Step title="The tangent plane (2D linearization)">
For $f(x, y)$ near a point $(a, b)$:

$$
f(x, y) \;\approx\; f(a, b) + f_x(a, b)\,(x - a) + f_y(a, b)\,(y - b).
$$

Same shape, two variables instead of one. The right-hand side is a **plane**, tilted by the partial derivatives and anchored at the height $f(a, b)$. Near $(a, b)$, the surface is indistinguishable from that plane.

Using the gradient notation from the last lesson:

$$
f(\mathbf{x}) \;\approx\; f(\mathbf{a}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}).
$$

This is *the* local linear model. Every step of gradient descent trusts this approximation for one tiny $\epsilon$, then reapproximates at the new point. Unreasonably effective.
</Step>

<Step title="Critical points">
A **critical point** is a point where the gradient is zero: $\nabla f(\mathbf{p}) = \mathbf{0}$.

In 1D, $f'(a) = 0$ meant "flat spot," a candidate for a local min or max. In 2D it means the same thing, except "flat" now means flat in *every* direction simultaneously. No direction is uphill; no direction is downhill.

Three shapes a 2D flat spot can take:

- A **local minimum** (bowl).
- A **local maximum** (cap).
- A **saddle** (up in some directions, down in others).

You found all three in step 1. Now formalize.
</Step>

<Step title="The second-derivative test">
Compute the Hessian discriminant at the critical point:

$$
D \;=\; f_{xx}\, f_{yy} \,-\, f_{xy}^{\,2}
$$

Then:

- $D > 0$ and $f_{xx} > 0$: **local minimum** (both axes curve up).
- $D > 0$ and $f_{xx} < 0$: **local maximum** (both curve down).
- $D < 0$: **saddle** (directions disagree).
- $D = 0$: the test is inconclusive. Probe by hand.

The matrix of second partials $\begin{pmatrix} f_{xx} & f_{xy} \\ f_{yx} & f_{yy} \end{pmatrix}$ is called the **Hessian**. We'll meet it as a matrix in Module 7. For now: sign of $D$, sign of $f_{xx}$. That's the test.
</Step>

<Step title="Why saddles matter for ML">
Intuition from one or two variables says: most flat spots are minima. Bowl, cap, occasional saddle. **In high dimensions this stops being true.** In dimensions ~millions (which is where neural-network loss functions live) saddles **dominate**. True local minima are rare; flat regions where the gradient is nearly zero but the function is actually a saddle are common.

This is why plain gradient descent can feel slow: it's not stuck at a real minimum, it's crawling through a high-dimensional saddle where the gradient is small in most directions and only a tiny sub-space escapes. Modules 10 and 11 cover the tricks for dealing with this: momentum, Adam, learning-rate schedules. All of them are about detecting and escaping saddles.

For now the single takeaway: **$\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$ doesn't mean "you won."** It means "stop and look more carefully."
</Step>
