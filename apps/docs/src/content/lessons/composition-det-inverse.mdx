<Step title="Multiplication as composition">
If $A$ is a transformation and $B$ is another transformation, then $AB$ is the transformation "first do $B$, then do $A$." Read right-to-left, like function composition $f(g(x))$.

$$
(AB)\mathbf{x} \;=\; A(B\mathbf{x}).
$$

The right-to-left order matters; it's the same convention as function composition from Module 5. It trips people up at first, because you write $AB$ but you apply $B$ first. Own it.
</Step>

<Step title="The columns of AB">
Given the column picture, there's a clean way to compute $AB$. Each column of $AB$ is $A$ applied to the corresponding column of $B$.

$$
AB \;=\; \bigl[\; A\,\mathbf{b}_1 \;\big|\; A\,\mathbf{b}_2 \;\big|\; \cdots \;\bigr].
$$

If $B$ sends $\hat{\imath}$ to $\mathbf{b}_1$, then $AB$ sends $\hat{\imath}$ to $A\mathbf{b}_1$, because "$A$ after $B$" first maps $\hat{\imath}$ to $\mathbf{b}_1$ via $B$, then maps $\mathbf{b}_1$ to $A\mathbf{b}_1$ via $A$. Column-picture all the way down.

The row-times-column formula $(AB)_{ij} = \sum_k A_{ik} B_{kj}$ gives the same answer. Same pattern as last lesson: column picture for meaning, rows for computation.
</Step>

<Step title="Determinant: the signed area factor">
Take any 2Ã—2 matrix $A$. Apply it to the unit square. The result is a parallelogram. Its **signed area** is called the determinant of $A$:

$$
\det A \;=\; ad - bc \;=\; \text{signed area of the parallelogram spanned by the columns}.
$$

"Signed" means: positive if the transformation preserves orientation, negative if it flips (a reflection, for example). A pure rotation has determinant $+1$. A reflection across an axis has determinant $-1$. A matrix that stretches the plane by a factor of 3 in both directions has determinant $9$.

Drag the columns on the widget above and watch the `det` readout. When the orientation flips, the color of the unit-square shape flips too. That's the sign doing its work.
</Step>

<Step title="Det = 0 means collapse">
When $\det A = 0$, the parallelogram flattens. The two columns lie on the same line; their span is 1-dimensional; the transformation has squashed 2D space into a line (or, if both columns are zero, into a single point).

Four equivalent statements (memorize the equivalence, not four separate facts):

- $\det A = 0$.
- The columns of $A$ are linearly dependent.
- The transformation's output span is lower-dimensional than its input.
- $A$ has no inverse.

Drag your widget columns onto the same line. All four things happen at once. The determinant hits zero, the rank badge reads 1, the unit-square shape degenerates to a segment, and, as the next step will show, no inverse exists because information has been destroyed.
</Step>

<Step title="Inverse as undoing">
The **inverse** of $A$, written $A^{-1}$, is the transformation that undoes $A$. Apply $A$ then $A^{-1}$ and you're back where you started.

$$
A \cdot A^{-1} \;=\; A^{-1} \cdot A \;=\; I.
$$

If $A$ rotates by 30Â°, $A^{-1}$ rotates by $-30Â°$. If $A$ scales by 2, $A^{-1}$ scales by $1/2$. Every invertible transformation has a unique "undo" operation.

But: if $A$ squashes space (say, it projects every point onto the $x$-axis) there is no undo. Points that used to live at different $y$-values got sent to the same place. You can't recover information that was thrown away. That's why **$A^{-1}$ exists if and only if $\det A \ne 0$**.

The formula for 2Ã—2 inverses (drop it in if you need to compute one):

$$
\begin{bmatrix}a & b \\ c & d\end{bmatrix}^{-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{ad - bc}\begin{bmatrix}d & -b \\ -c & a\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Note the $ad - bc$ in the denominator: it's the determinant again. When it's zero, the formula blows up. That's the algebra telling you what the geometry already said.
</Step>
