<Step title="The pattern hiding in x², x³, x⁴">
You already have two derivatives, earned the long way through limits:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\,x^2 \;=\; 2x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\,x^3 \;=\; 3x^2
$$

Look at the exponents. Two becomes one. Three becomes two. The old exponent comes out the front as a multiplier. If the pattern holds, then

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\,x^4 \;=\; 4x^3 \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\,x^5 \;=\; 5x^4.
$$

It holds. Not just for whole numbers either: it holds for $-1$, for $1/2$, for $\pi$.
</Step>

<Step title="A picture for why">
Take the square with side $x$. Its area is $x^2$. Now grow the side by a tiny amount $dx$. The new area is $(x + dx)^2$, and the *change* in area is everything that just got added: two thin strips along the existing sides, each of size $x \cdot dx$, plus a tiny corner square of size $dx \cdot dx$.

$$
(x + dx)^2 - x^2 \;=\; 2 x\,dx \;+\; (dx)^2.
$$

Divide by $dx$ to get the rate of change of area per unit of side:

$$
\frac{(x + dx)^2 - x^2}{dx} \;=\; 2x \;+\; dx.
$$

Send $dx \to 0$. The corner square vanishes; the two strips survive. The rate is $2x$. That is $d/dx\,x^2$, derived from a *picture* instead of an algebraic limit.

The same picture works in three dimensions for $x^3$: a cube grows three thin slabs of area $x^2$, giving $3 x^2$. In general, $x^n$ grows $n$ "slabs" each of size $x^{n-1}$, hence $n \cdot x^{n-1}$.
</Step>

<Step title="The power rule">
For any real number $n$:

$$
\boxed{\;\frac{d}{dx}\,x^n \;=\; n\,x^{n-1}\;}
$$

That is one line. It handles every power you will ever take, positive or negative, integer or fraction or irrational.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dx}\,\frac{1}{x} \;=\; \frac{d}{dx}\,x^{-1} \;&=\; -1 \cdot x^{-2} \;=\; -\frac{1}{x^2} \\[3pt]
\frac{d}{dx}\,\sqrt{x} \;=\; \frac{d}{dx}\,x^{1/2} \;&=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,x^{-1/2} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}
\end{aligned}
$$

No new tricks. The same shape, every time.
</Step>

<Step title="Constants and sums">
Two rules so obvious they barely need a name. The derivative is **linear**: it slides through addition, and it slides past constant multipliers.

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[c\bigr] = 0 \qquad
\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[c \cdot f(x)\bigr] = c \cdot f'(x) \qquad
\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[f(x) + g(x)\bigr] = f'(x) + g'(x).
$$

In words: a constant has zero slope (it never changes), a scalar multiplier passes straight through, and the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives.

Combine those with the power rule and you can differentiate any polynomial *by inspection*:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\bigl(3x^4 - 5x^2 + 7\bigr) \;=\; 12 x^3 - 10 x.
$$

The $7$ dies (it is a constant). Each remaining term loses one exponent and picks up the old exponent as a coefficient.
</Step>

<Step title="Sine and cosine, derived from the unit circle">
Two more derivatives, neither of which you can get from the power rule:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\,\sin x \;=\; \cos x \qquad \frac{d}{dx}\,\cos x \;=\; -\sin x.
$$

You already *saw* this in the previous lesson when you swept across $\sin x$ on the tracer and the slope-curve was $\cos x$. The geometric reason: $\sin$ and $\cos$ are the height and width of a point spinning on the unit circle. A tiny rotation $dt$ moves that point by $dt$ along the circle, perpendicular to its current direction. The change in height (i.e., in $\sin$) is $\cos(\text{angle}) \cdot dt$. The change in width (in $\cos$) is $-\sin(\text{angle}) \cdot dt$. The minus sign is *the same minus sign* you see in the formula.

If you want the full geometric derivation, it lives in module 3. For now the two formulas are tools to use.
</Step>

<Step title="The exponential e is the function that is its own derivative">
There is one function in mathematics whose derivative is *itself*:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\,e^x \;=\; e^x.
$$

That is the property that distinguishes $e \approx 2.71828\ldots$ from every other base. For any other base $b$,

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\,b^x \;=\; (\ln b) \cdot b^x.
$$

For $b = 2$, the multiplier $\ln 2 \approx 0.693$. For $b = 3$, $\ln 3 \approx 1.099$. There is exactly *one* base where the multiplier is exactly $1$, and the derivative becomes the function itself. That base is $e$.

<Step title="The logarithm">
One more, also new:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\,\ln x \;=\; \frac{1}{x}, \qquad x > 0.
$$

The justification will be cleanest after we have the chain rule, but the intuition is short. The natural logarithm $\ln$ is the inverse of $e^x$. Inverses reflect across the line $y = x$, which swaps "input" and "output" everywhere. If $e^x$ at the point $(0, 1)$ has slope $1$, then $\ln$ at the reflected point $(1, 0)$ also has slope $1$. Repeat at $(\ln 2, 2)$ on the exponential — slope $2$ — and at the reflected point $(2, \ln 2)$ on the logarithm — slope $1/2$. The slopes are reciprocals of each other. At $x$, $\ln$ has slope $1/x$.
</Step>

<Step title="What you have">
Seven differentiation rules. With these, you can take the derivative of every elementary function you will meet in this course *except* those built by multiplying functions together or composing them. Those two cases — products and compositions — get their own rules next.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{d}{dx}[c] &= 0 & \frac{d}{dx}[x^n] &= n\,x^{n-1} \\[3pt]
\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] &= \cos x & \frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] &= -\sin x \\[3pt]
\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] &= e^x & \frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] &= \frac{1}{x}
\end{aligned}
$$

And the sum/scalar rules that glue them together. Most of the "differentiation" that runs inside a neural-network library is, ultimately, these seven formulas applied a trillion times.
</Step>
