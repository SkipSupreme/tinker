<Step title="Spot the pattern">
Two derivatives you've already earned:

$$
\frac{d}{dx} x^2 \;=\; 2x
\qquad
\frac{d}{dx} x^3 \;=\; 3x^2
$$

One you haven't (yet):

$$
\frac{d}{dx} x^4 \;=\; \;?
$$

Look at the pattern. The exponent drops by one. The old exponent comes out front as a multiplier. If that's right:

$$
\frac{d}{dx} x^4 \;=\; 4 x^3.
$$

It is right. And it generalizes.
</Step>

<Step title="The power rule">
For any real number $n$:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\, x^n \;=\; n \cdot x^{n-1}.
$$

This one line handles every polynomial you'll ever meet. Combined with the fact that derivatives of sums are sums of derivatives and derivatives of scalar multiples are scaled derivatives, you can differentiate any polynomial by inspection:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\big(7x^5 - 3x^2 + 4x - 11\big) \;=\; 35x^4 - 6x + 4.
$$

The constant $-11$ dies (its derivative is zero; it doesn't change with $x$). The linear term $4x$ becomes $4$. Every other term loses one exponent and picks up a coefficient.

Negative and fractional exponents work too. $x^{-1} = 1/x$, so $d/dx\,(1/x) = -x^{-2} = -1/x^2$. And $\sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$, so $d/dx\,\sqrt{x} = \tfrac{1}{2} x^{-1/2} = 1/(2\sqrt{x})$.
</Step>

<Step title="Why the pattern holds">
We derived $d/dx\,x^2 = 2x$ by the long road in the first lesson: secant line, limit, algebra. The same argument scales.

Expand $(x + h)^n$ using the binomial theorem, subtract $x^n$, divide by $h$, let $h \to 0$. The first two terms of the binomial expansion are $x^n$ and $n\,x^{n-1}\,h$; everything after has at least $h^2$ and dies in the limit. What's left is $n\,x^{n-1}$.

You don't need to internalize that proof; just know the rule isn't magic. It's the same shrink-the-secant story from the first lesson, done once in general and then applied a trillion times.
</Step>

<Step title="Where it shows up">
Every neural network layer contains matrix multiplications and nonlinearities, and many of those nonlinearities are simple powers: $\mathrm{ReLU}(x) = \max(0, x)$ is a piecewise power, squared-error loss uses $x^2$, regularization penalties often use $x^2$ or $|x|$. When backpropagation computes the gradient through these, the power rule is what's running.

You now have three tools: the definition of the derivative, the chain rule, and the power rule. Together they cover about 80% of the derivatives you'll take for the rest of this course. The other 20% (trig, exponentials, logs) is the next module.
</Step>
