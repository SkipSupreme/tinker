<Step title="The derivative of the derivative">
$f'$ is a function. It has its own slope. The slope of $f'$ is called the **second derivative** of $f$, written $f''$ or $d^2 y / dx^2$.

$$
f''(x) \;=\; \frac{d}{dx}\bigl[f'(x)\bigr].
$$

The physical picture is the cleanest. If $f(t)$ is the **position** of a moving object at time $t$, then $f'(t)$ is its **velocity** (how fast position is changing), and $f''(t)$ is its **acceleration** (how fast velocity is changing). Position-velocity-acceleration is exactly position-first derivative-second derivative.

You compute $f''$ by differentiating twice. For $f(x) = x^3$:

$$
f'(x) = 3x^2, \qquad f''(x) = 6x.
$$
</Step>

<Step title="What f'' tells you that f' doesn't">
$f'$ tells you which way the curve is going at $x$: positive slope means up, negative means down. $f''$ tells you which way that *direction* is bending:

- $f'' > 0$: the slope is *increasing*. The curve bends upward. **Concave up** — shaped like a cup.
- $f'' < 0$: the slope is *decreasing*. The curve bends downward. **Concave down** — shaped like a cap.
- $f'' = 0$ and changes sign: the bend itself flips. That point is called an **inflection point**.

For $f(x) = x^2$, $f''(x) = 2 > 0$ everywhere — always concave up, always a cup. For $f(x) = -x^2$, $f''(x) = -2 < 0$ everywhere — always a cap. For $f(x) = x^3$, $f''(x) = 6x$ flips sign at $0$: concave down for $x < 0$, concave up for $x > 0$, with $0$ as the inflection point.
</Step>

<Step title="Critical points: where the slope is zero">
A **critical point** of $f$ is a value $x$ where $f'(x) = 0$ (or where $f'$ does not exist). Geometrically these are the points where the tangent is horizontal — the candidates for peaks and valleys.

Drag the marker below. The widget shows $f$ in red, $f'$ in faint teal, and the marker turns green when you land on a critical point. Hunt for all of them on each function.

<CriticalPointHunt client:visible />

For $x^3 - 3x$: two critical points at $x = \pm 1$. For $x^4 - 4x^2$: three, at $x = 0, \pm\sqrt{2}$. For $x^3$: just one, at $0$ — and the verdict will tell you something interesting.

That last one is the warning. A critical point is *not the same as* an extremum. $f(x) = x^3$ has $f'(0) = 0$, but $x = 0$ is neither a max nor a min — the curve is just briefly flat as it passes through. You need more than $f' = 0$ to classify what the point actually is.
</Step>

<Step title="Two tests, one job">
Given a critical point $c$ (so $f'(c) = 0$), how do you tell whether it is a max, a min, or neither?

**The first-derivative test.** Look at $f'$ on either side of $c$.

- $f'$ goes from $+$ to $-$ as $x$ crosses $c$ → **local maximum** (climbing, then descending).
- $f'$ goes from $-$ to $+$ → **local minimum** (descending, then climbing).
- $f'$ keeps the same sign on both sides → **neither** (the curve flattens briefly and continues).

**The second-derivative test.** Compute $f''(c)$.

- $f''(c) > 0$ → concave up at $c$ → **local minimum**.
- $f''(c) < 0$ → concave down at $c$ → **local maximum**.
- $f''(c) = 0$ → inconclusive. Fall back to the first-derivative test.

The two agree when they both apply, and the second is usually faster. The widget runs the second-derivative test for you each time you land.
</Step>

<Step title="Why any of this matters: minimization is training">
Take a single sentence and read it slowly:

> Training a neural network is finding the values of its weights that minimize a loss function.

That sentence is a calculus problem. The "loss function" is a function from millions of inputs (the weights) to a single output (a number measuring how badly the network is performing). "Minimize" means: find the critical point where the gradient — the multi-dimensional cousin of $f'$ — is zero, and use the multi-dimensional cousin of $f''$ to confirm it is a minimum and not a saddle.

Module 10 will make this picture quantitative. Module 12 will give you the algorithm for computing the gradient through any function you can write down — that is backpropagation. Both of them are this lesson, generalized to many variables.
</Step>
